Pediatric MRI Head Phantom

Research Engineer • 2025 • The Martino Center - Mass General Brigham

MRI head phantoms are 3D printed models that get filled with a solution to mimic brain MR signal and conductivity for research.

Working with Jason Stockmann at the Martinos Center, I developed a pediatric head phantom to support MRI research focused on improving imaging for pediatric care.

In previous research, imaging patients with braces caused consistent problems. The metal wires create magnetic field distortions, so parts of the scan end up blurry or completely missing.

This phantom is used as a controlled way to study those artifacts and test better magnetic field mapping to improve the image.

I started with the "Angel 1" head phantom mesh from the Martinos Center phantom wiki and iterated on the design. I added an extruded section that protrudes from the skull so metal brace wires and brackets can be placed on it.

I optimized the spacing between the extrusion and the outer wall so it produced realistic artifact behavior while still sealing properly and preventing leaks.

This pediatric head phantom is still being used in ongoing research. It's helping the team test field mapping and correction methods, and the results have shown improved image quality when scanning cases affected by braces.

Low-Field MRI Coil Remapping and Connection Integration

Research Engineer • 2025 • The Martino Center - Mass General Brigham

Updated the wiring for a low-field AC/DC MRI coil developed by Jason Stockmann, PhD, to support new ODU connectors for connecting to the shim amplifier.

To implement this, I redesigned the wiring layout and used CAD to model a mounting piece that holds the female ODU connector and securely attaches it to the coil body.

Each wire was cut to length and resoldered to the appropriate ODU pin so it could be installed into the new connector port.

The main connector opening was machined using a mill, and the CAD mount was designed to take advantage of the existing connection cutouts so the new connector port could integrate cleanly with the coil's original housing.

The CAD design was optimized to fit seamlessly with the existing coil structure, ensuring a secure and reliable connection while maintaining the coil's functionality.

As well as this, an F-to-M connection was required, and the previous cable end was loose and easy to damage. I designed an Onshape CAD model to both protect the cable and make it easier to assemble and use.

The part was a 3-section symmetrical design with dedicated wire slots, so each wire could be placed in the correct position and the cable could be inserted in a simple, consistent way every time.

I also added a ridge at the end of the model that clamps down on the cable, keeping the housing secure and preventing the cable from shifting or pulling loose.

The 3D printed housing provided strain relief and organized wire management, significantly improving the durability and usability of the connector assembly.

Iterated on an existing PCB design in KiCad and redesigned the wiring to support a new 8-wire ribbon cable interface. This allowed quick switching between two different coils that required different wiring configurations, without rewiring the system each time.

I sourced and verified all required components through Digi-Key and Mouser, assembled and soldered the full board, and integrated it into the shim amplifier for testing and use.

The final PCB assembly successfully enabled seamless coil switching and improved the overall workflow efficiency for the research team.
